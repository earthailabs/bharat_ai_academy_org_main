Chapter 1

What is a Computer?

Before we learn AI, we must be comfortable with the machine AI runs on — the computer.

A computer is a machine that can store information, process information, and show you results. That's it. Everything else — games, videos, AI — is built on these three jobs.

💡 Analogy — The Kitchen

Think of a computer as a kitchen. The fridge stores ingredients (storage). The cook prepares the dish (processor). The plate shows you the finished food (screen). You give the order (keyboard and mouse) — the kitchen does the work.

The main parts

PartWhat it doesKitchen analogy
Processor (CPU)Does the thinking and calculationsThe cook
Memory (RAM)Holds what you are working on right nowThe kitchen counter
Storage (Hard disk / SSD)Keeps your files safe, even when power is offThe fridge
ScreenShows you everythingThe plate
Keyboard & MouseHow you give instructionsPlacing your order
🔑 AI Term — Hardware & Software

Hardware = the parts you can touch (screen, keyboard, mouse).
Software = the programs that run on it (WhatsApp, Chrome, games). You cannot touch software — it is instructions for the hardware.

Types of computers you already know

💻

Laptop

A portable computer. Screen + keyboard in one. This is what you will use in class.

🖥️

Desktop

A bigger computer that stays on a desk. Separate screen, keyboard, and box.

📱

Smartphone

Yes — your phone is a computer too! It stores, processes, and shows information.

☁️

Server

A powerful computer in a big building far away. When you use ChatGPT, a server does the AI work.

📝 Remember This

Computer = Store + Process + Show. Your phone, your laptop, and the giant machines running ChatGPT all follow this same idea.

What is an Operating System?

When you switch on a laptop, a special software wakes up first and manages everything. It is called the Operating System (OS).

  • Windows — most common on laptops in India. We will use this.
  • macOS — runs on Apple laptops (MacBook).
  • Android — runs on most phones.
  • iOS — runs on iPhones.
💡 Analogy — The School Principal

The Operating System is like a school principal. Students (apps) do the actual work, but the principal decides who gets which classroom (memory), keeps the timetable (schedules tasks), and keeps everything running smoothly.

🎯 Try It Now

On your laptop, press the Windows key (the key with the window logo, bottom-left). The Start Menu opens — this is the front door of your Operating System. Press it again to close.

The Desktop, Mouse & Keyboard

The screen you see after your laptop starts is called the Desktop. Let's learn its parts.

Parts of the screen

NameWhereWhat it is
DesktopThe whole screenYour work table. Icons sit on it.
IconSmall picturesA shortcut to open an app or file. Double-click to open.
TaskbarBottom stripShows open apps, the clock, and the Start button.
Start MenuBottom-leftThe list of all apps on the computer.
WindowAny open boxEvery app opens inside a rectangle called a window.

Mouse actions — the five moves

  • Click (left button, once) — select something.
  • Double-click (left button, twice quickly) — open something.
  • Right-click (right button, once) — open a menu of options. Very useful!
  • Drag — press and hold left button, move the mouse, release. Moves things.
  • Scroll — roll the wheel to move up and down a page.
⚠️ Common Mistake

Beginners often double-click everything. Remember: one click = select, two clicks = open. Buttons on websites need only one click.

🎯 Try It Now

Right-click on an empty area of your Desktop. See the menu that appears? This menu changes depending on where you right-click. Right-click is your best friend in Windows.

Chapter 3

Files & Folders

This is the most important chapter of Part 1. Everything in AI work depends on understanding files and folders.

Start with a question

Your phone has hundreds of photos. Where do they live?

They are stored inside your phone's memory, arranged in folders — one folder for camera photos, one for WhatsApp images, one for screenshots. Your laptop works exactly the same way.

💡 Analogy — The Cupboard

Your computer is a cupboard.
Each shelf in the cupboard is a folder.
Each book on a shelf is a file.
A shelf can also hold a smaller box (a folder inside a folder!), and that box can hold more books.

CupboardComputer
→
ShelfFolder
→
BookFile
🔑 AI Term — File

A file is one single item saved on a computer: one photo, one document, one song, one video. Every file has a name and a type.

🔑 AI Term — Folder

A folder is a container that holds files and other folders. Folders keep your computer organized, just like shelves keep a cupboard organized.

Folders inside folders

A folder can contain more folders. This creates a tree — like branches of a family. Here is an example of a student's computer:

Documents ├── College │ ├── Notes │ │ ├── physics-notes.pdf │ │ └── maths-notes.pdf │ └── Assignments │ └── essay.docx ├── Photos │ └── trip-jaipur.jpg └── AI Workspace └── (we will build this in class!)

Reading the tree above: the Documents folder contains three folders. The College folder contains two more folders. The file physics-notes.pdf lives inside Notes, which lives inside College, which lives inside Documents.

🔑 AI Term — Path

The path is the address of a file — the full route to reach it. Example:
Documents → College → Notes → physics-notes.pdf
Just like a home address: State → City → Street → House.

📝 Remember This

Folders hold things. Files are the things. A folder is never "opened in an app" — it just shows what's inside. A file always opens in some app (photo app, PDF reader, browser).

🎯 Try It Now

Open File Explorer (yellow folder icon in the taskbar, or press Windows + E). Click on Documents on the left side. Look at what folders and files already live there.

File Types — The Surname of Every File

Every file name has two parts: the name you choose, and a short ending that tells the computer what kind of file it is.

💡 Analogy — Name and Surname

A file is like a person. resume.pdf — here resume is the first name (you choose it), and .pdf is the surname (it tells the family — the file type). Just as a surname tells you which family someone belongs to, the extension tells the computer which app should open the file.

🔑 AI Term — Extension

The extension is the part of the file name after the dot: .pdf, .jpg, .txt. It tells the computer (and AI) what type of content is inside.

⚠️ Common Mistake

By default, Windows hides extensions! You see "resume" instead of "resume.pdf". This confuses beginners badly. We will fix this in the next chapter — it is the first thing every AI student should do on a new laptop.

Can AI read these files?

Modern AI tools (like Cursor, ChatGPT, Gemini) can read most files:

  • Reads easily: .txt, .md, .html, .css, .js, .json
  • Reads well: .pdf, .docx, .xlsx — AI extracts the text inside
  • Can see: .jpg, .png — AI can look at images and describe them
  • Cannot read directly: .zip — you must unpack it first

Chapter 5

Windows Skills — Hands On

Time to practise. Do every step on your laptop as you read. These small skills will save you hours later.

Skill 1 — Show file extensions (do this first!)

  • Open File Explorer (press Windows + E).
  • Click View in the top menu.
  • Find and tick File name extensions. (On Windows 11: View → Show → File name extensions.)
  • Now every file shows its full name, like notes.txt instead of just "notes".

Skill 2 — Create a new folder

  • Go to your Desktop (or open Documents).
  • Right-click on an empty area.
  • Choose New → Folder.
  • Type a name, for example Practice Folder, and press Enter.

Skill 3 — Rename a file or folder

  • Click the file once to select it.
  • Press F2 on the keyboard.
  • Type the new name and press Enter.
⚠️ Common Mistake

When renaming, do not delete the extension. If you rename photo.jpg to just photo and remove .jpg, Windows may not know how to open it. Keep the surname!

Skill 4 — Copy and paste a file

  • Click the file once to select it.
  • Press Ctrl + C (copy). Nothing visible happens — the file is remembered.
  • Open the folder where you want the copy.
  • Press Ctrl + V (paste). A copy appears!

Skill 5 — Move a file (cut and paste)

  • Select the file, press Ctrl + X (cut).
  • Open the destination folder.
  • Press Ctrl + V. The file has moved — it is no longer in the old place.
📝 Remember This

Copy = original stays + a duplicate is made.
Cut = the file moves to the new place.
Made a mistake? Press Ctrl + Z to undo it immediately!

Skill 6 — Delete and recover

  • Select the file, press Delete. It goes to the Recycle Bin — not gone forever.
  • Deleted by mistake? Open Recycle Bin (Desktop icon), right-click the file → Restore.

Skill 7 — Search for a file

  • Press the Windows key and simply start typing the file name.
  • Or, inside File Explorer, use the search box at the top-right.

Create this structure on your Desktop, using only the skills above:

Practice Folder containing: Notes.txt (right-click → New → Text Document), a copy of any photo renamed to Image.jpg, and a folder inside it called My Work. Then move Notes.txt into My Work.

The three folders you must know

Windows gives every user three special folders. Knowing the difference prevents the most common beginner confusion: "Where did my file go?!"

🖥️

Desktop

What you see when the laptop starts. Good for shortcuts and current work. Don't let it become a junkyard!

⬇️

Downloads

Everything you download from the internet lands here automatically. Clean it every week.

📄

Documents

The best home for long-term work. Your AI Workspace will live here.

💡 Analogy — Rooms of a House

Desktop = the dining table — things you're using right now.
Downloads = the doormat — everything arriving from outside lands here first.
Documents = the almirah — where important things are kept properly, long-term.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Saving everything in Downloads and never moving it. Downloads becomes a jungle of 500 files, and nothing can be found. Rule: download → move to the right folder → keep Downloads clean.

Chapter 6

Local vs Cloud vs Browser

Three words people mix up all the time. After this chapter, you never will.

Local — on YOUR machine

A local file lives on your laptop's own storage. No internet needed to open it. If your Wi-Fi goes off, the file still works. If your laptop is lost, the file is lost with it (unless you made a copy).

Cloud — on SOMEONE ELSE'S machine

A cloud file lives on a big company's server — a powerful computer in a data centre far away. Google Drive, Google Photos, and OneDrive are cloud services. You need internet to reach these files, but you can open them from any device.

💡 Analogy — Almirah vs Bank Locker

Local = your almirah at home. Fast to open, works anytime — but if something happens to the house, it's gone.
Cloud = a bank locker. Your things are stored safely in the bank's building. You need to travel there (internet) to access them — but even if your house has a problem, your locker is safe. And you can visit from any branch (any device).

Browser — the WINDOW, not the cupboard

Chrome and Edge are browsers. A browser is an app that opens websites. It does not store your files — it is the window through which you look at the internet.

🔑 AI Term — Cloud

The cloud simply means "someone else's powerful computers, reachable through the internet". There is no actual cloud in the sky — just huge buildings full of servers.

Why this matters for AI

  • When you chat with ChatGPT, your question travels through the browser to the cloud, where the AI thinks, and the answer travels back.
  • When you use Cursor on your laptop, it reads your local files — your folders and projects — and sends questions to AI in the cloud.
  • Knowing where your files are = knowing what AI can and cannot see.
🎯 Try It Now

Turn off your laptop's Wi-Fi for one minute. Try opening: (1) a file in Documents, (2) google.com in Chrome. Which one still works? That's the difference between local and cloud!

Internet Basics

You use the internet every day on your phone. Let's understand it just enough to work smartly with AI.

What is the internet?

The internet is millions of computers connected to each other around the world. When you open a website, your device asks another computer (a server) for that page, and the server sends it back — all in less than a second.

💡 Analogy — The Postal System

The internet works like a super-fast postal system. You write a request (open a website), it travels through many post offices (network cables and towers), reaches the right address (the server), and the reply is delivered back to you — in milliseconds instead of days.

Words you hear every day

WordSimple meaning
WebsiteA place on the internet, like google.com
URL / LinkThe address of a website or page
Search engineA website that finds other websites (Google)
DownloadBringing a file from the internet to your laptop
UploadSending a file from your laptop to the internet
Wi-FiInternet without wires, over radio signals
Account / LoginYour personal identity on a website — email + password
⚠️ Common Mistake — Downloading from wrong buttons

Many websites show big fake "DOWNLOAD" buttons that are actually advertisements. The real download link is usually smaller and matches the website's design. When in doubt, ask your instructor.
